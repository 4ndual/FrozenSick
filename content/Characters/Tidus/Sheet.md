## Backstory

I became an orphan early. When I was fifteen, the southern elven tribe—my people—was destroyed. Half of us were killed in a war we didn't lose by force, but by betrayal. Allies failed us when it mattered, and the cost was paid in lives.

I survived by being moved north, taken in by another elven tribe. They gave me shelter, but not belonging. I was tolerated, not trusted. That was where I learned my first real lesson: strength isn't always enough, and honesty isn't always useful. If I wanted to survive—if I wanted control—I had to learn how to speak, how to lie, how to persuade without being seen as a threat.

Over time, that skill opened doors that strength never could.

Through circumstance and usefulness rather than birth or honor, I became involved with dragonborn royalty. They didn't need another warrior. They needed someone who could move quietly, act without banners, and handle problems that couldn't be solved openly. I traveled with their forces for years, working alongside disciplined soldiers while never truly being one of them. In that time, I built contacts, favors, and an understanding of how power actually moves.

Eventually, one mission went wrong. Badly. Wounded and unable to return south or north, I sought refuge in a dwarven city. They didn't ask many questions. They value actions over stories. They kept me alive, hid me when it mattered, and later gave me a chance to repay the debt. I did—and I never forgot it.

I don't reject being an elf. I accept it. I can stand among my people without resentment. But my strongest bonds are not tribal. I trust dwarves because they remember debts, and I work well with dragonborn because they understand discipline and consequence.

I've learned to live between cultures, between laws, and between wars—doing what needs to be done quietly, even when it costs me.
